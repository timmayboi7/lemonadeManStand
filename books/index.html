The Compliance Office

The Compliance Office

2024 · Literary Fiction, Workplace, Bureaucracy

Maya Chen has worked in compliance for eleven years. She knows which forms require signatures in triplicate, which violations merit a warning versus a fine, and how to navigate the software system that crashes every Tuesday at 2 PM. She does not know why the new directive requires her to flag cases she would have previously approved.

The directive comes without explanation. The recipients are not told its purpose, only its procedure. Maya follows it. Her colleagues follow it. Someone, somewhere, benefits from the pattern of denials that begins to emerge. By the time Maya understands what she has helped build, the structure is too large to dismantle alone.

The Compliance Office is a novel about administrative violence—the harm done not by individuals but by systems that distribute responsibility so thinly that no one person can be held accountable. It is also about the moment when following procedure becomes complicity.

Quiet Zones

Quiet Zones

2024 · Literary Fiction, Community, Gentrification

The neighborhood was never quiet, but it was loud in ways residents understood. The arguments in Spanish from the apartment below. The music from the barbershop on the corner. The children in the courtyard until streetlights came on. Then the developers arrived with a new definition of quiet.

Quiet Zones follows three residents of a changing neighborhood over the course of one year. There is Elena, who has lived in the same building for forty years and now receives monthly offers to sell. There is Marcus, who opened a restaurant before the area was desirable and now cannot afford the new rent. There is Priya, who moved in six months ago and is already attending community meetings about "quality of life" issues.

None of them are villains. None are heroes. All are making rational choices within a system that rewards displacement and calls it progress. The novel asks what community means when the ground beneath it keeps shifting.

The Inheritance

The Inheritance

2024 · Literary Fiction, Family, Class

When David Voss dies, he leaves his three children a house they cannot afford to keep, debts they did not know existed, and a set of instructions that reveal how little they understood their father. The will is not the document that matters. The document that matters is the one they find in a safety deposit box, written in a language none of them speak.

The Inheritance traces three siblings as they attempt to settle their father's affairs and confront the legacy he actually left behind. The oldest, Richard, believes in order and procedure. The middle child, Sarah, believes in cutting losses. The youngest, Thomas, believes in finding meaning where none may exist. Their conflicts are not about the estate. They are about what they owe to the dead and what they owe to themselves.

The novel examines how wealth and its absence shape families across generations. It asks whether it is possible to inherit only what you choose to keep.
