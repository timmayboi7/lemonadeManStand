On Writing Unlikable Characters

January 15, 2024 · 5 min read

Readers want someone to root for. I give them someone to understand. The distinction matters more than most writers admit.

A character does not need to be good to be compelling. They need to be comprehensible. The reader should finish a chapter able to explain why the character acted as they did, even if they would have acted differently themselves. This is the contract: not agreement, but understanding.

The unlikable character is a test. Does the reader's sympathy extend only to people who behave well? Or can it encompass those who behave badly for reasons the reader recognizes in themselves?

The Problem with Hope

January 8, 2024 · 4 min read

Hope is not optimism. Hope is the refusal to look away. In fiction, it is often the only honest ending available.

Optimism believes things will get better. Hope acknowledges they might not—and chooses to continue anyway. This distinction matters for writers working in dark material. An optimistic ending feels false. A hopeful ending feels earned.

The difference lies in what the characters know. Optimistic characters do not see the obstacles. Hopeful characters see them clearly and persist regardless. The reader trusts the latter because the reader has also persisted through difficulty without guarantee of outcome.

Research as Distraction

January 1, 2024 · 3 min read

I have spent three days reading about municipal zoning laws for a single paragraph. This is either dedication or avoidance.

Research serves two functions in fiction. It provides the details that make a world feel real. It also provides an excuse not to write. The challenge is knowing which function is currently operating.

When the research stops informing the story and starts replacing it, it has become procrastination. The test is simple: can I write the scene without this information? If yes, the research is delay. If no, it is necessary. The difficulty is answering honestly.

What I Learned from Editing

December 20, 2023 · 6 min read

The first draft is for discovering what you meant to say. The second draft is for discovering whether you actually said it. Everything after is negotiation.

Editing is where the writer confronts the gap between intention and execution. The sentence that felt profound in the moment of writing often reads as heavy-handed the next morning. The scene that seemed essential reveals itself as exposition in disguise.

The editor's question is always the same: what is this doing here? If the answer is not immediately clear, the material is suspect. Clarity is not the enemy of complexity. It is its vehicle.

Reading Against the Grain

December 10, 2023 · 4 min read

I read books I disagree with. Not to argue with them, but to understand what they assume—about people, about society, about what stories are worth telling.

Every novel carries a worldview. Most carry it unconsciously, embedded in what the characters notice and what they ignore, in what the plot rewards and what it punishes. Reading critically means excavating these assumptions, even in work you admire.

The books that change me are not the ones that confirm what I already believe. They are the ones that make me question why I believe it.

The Myth of the Solitary Writer

December 1, 2023 · 5 min read

Writing is solitary. Publishing is not. The confusion between these two activities creates unnecessary suffering for many writers.

The draft requires isolation. No one can write your sentences for you. But the decision to pursue publication, to seek readers, to participate in a literary conversation—this is social. It requires community, even if that community is asynchronous and distributed.

The romantic image of the writer alone in a room obscures the networks that make the work possible: the readers who provide feedback, the editors who shape the manuscript, the booksellers who place it in hands. Acknowledging this does not diminish the work. It places it in context.
